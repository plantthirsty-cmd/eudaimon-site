The science behind Eudaimon

The principles

Eudaimon isn't a willpower hack. It's built on a handful of hard truths about how the evening brain actually works — drawn from neuroscience and behavioral psychology — and every one of them is wired into the device. Here's the whole thinking, in order.

01

Willpower is a battery — and the night runs it flat

Self-control runs largely on the prefrontal cortex, just behind your forehead. Every decision across a day draws it down, and by late evening it's nearly empty — the exact moment most apps ask you to decide to stop. Eudaimon doesn't ask the tired brain to decide at all; the decision was already made this morning.

02

Decide once, while you're clear

Odysseus had himself tied to the mast before he could hear the sirens. Pre-commitment — a Ulysses contract — is one of the most robust findings in behavioral science: your clear-headed self sets the rules, and something outside you holds them when the depleted self would cave. Eudaimon is that something.

03

A commitment your body makes is the only kind that holds

An intention you can undo with a thumb isn't a commitment. The box physically detects your phone — no checkbox, no honour system — so docking it becomes a small, real act with weight. That physical ritual is what tells your brain the day is closing, and what makes the promise actually stick.

04

It can't live on the thing it's fighting

The cue and the craving sit on the same screen. Any app that polices your phone has a built-in conflict of interest — it lives one swipe away from exactly what you're resisting. Eudaimon is a separate object across the room: no feed, nothing to sell you, nothing to lose when you put the phone down.

05

In the danger zone, loss moves you more than gain

People work far harder to avoid a loss than to chase an equal gain — loss aversion, one of the best-documented effects in the field. So when you start to slip, Eudaimon doesn't sell you tomorrow's productivity; it shows you what you're losing right now: the sleep, the morning, the tomorrow you already chose.

06

Pressure you can ignore becomes wallpaper

The brain habituates to anything constant — nag it every night and it stops registering. So most nights Eudaimon stays dark and silent. Friction appears only the moment you actually start to slip, the one moment it can still mean something.

07

A little social weight, only if you want it

Knowing one person will notice is often the difference between meaning it and doing it. You can name a single trusted someone who gets a quiet note if you miss your handover — and nothing else. No usage, no location, no scoreboards, no shame. Entirely opt-in.

08

The point is relief, not discipline

Put all of it together and the nightly decision simply disappears — and with it, the nightly struggle. The feeling testers report most isn't willpower or pride. It's relief. That's not a side effect; it's the whole design.

Eudaimon is co-developed with neuroscience researchers and psychology students at HU Berlin — the science here isn't decoration, it's the blueprint.
