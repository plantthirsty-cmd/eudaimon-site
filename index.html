The problem

The version of you at 11pm is not the same person who set your alarm at 8am. They share a body but not a will.

Your willpower runs on the prefrontal cortex — the part of your brain right behind your forehead, where decisions and self-control are made. It works like a battery, and by late evening it's almost flat. Most sleep and habit apps fail because they ask your most drained self to make the hard call, at the exact moment it can't.

Eudaimon flips it

Morning-you makes the rules. The box holds them when evening-you would break them.

A Ulysses contract, in hardware — you tie yourself to the mast while you're still clear-headed, and the box keeps the rope when the song starts.

When it comes to improving yourself, 80% of it is just getting to bed on time.

Sleep is the lever under everything else. Win the night and the day mostly takes care of itself.

The night, walked through

It only appears when you need it. Doing fine? It stays dark and quiet.

01

Wind-down

One gentle ping as the evening begins. Ease off now — not a buzzer, not a nag. A single soft chime you can acknowledge with a tap.

02

The evening routine

A short checklist you wrote yourself, clear-headed. Each item marked negotiable or non-negotiable — so on a hard night you skip the rest and just do what matters.

03

The phone goes in the box

You place your phone in a real box that physically detects when it's inside. No tap or checkbox can stand in for it — the commitment is physical, and you can't quietly talk yourself out of it.

04

If you stall, it shifts tone

It doesn't just get louder. It moves from gentle, to firm, to your own promise — in your own words — filling the screen. There is no "comply" button. You finish by actually doing the thing.

05

Morning gives it back

Light rises before the alarm. A small cognitive task — never math — proves you're actually up, with no snooze to tap your way out of. Then the phone is earned back, and the day is yours.

Protective, not punitive

The sheepdog you hired this morning.

Not a cop. It catches you, but it never judges you, and it never tells you that you failed. When things are going fine, it stays dark and quiet and out of your way.

And the thing testers mention most isn't willpower. It's relief from the decision — the calm of having already chosen, hours earlier, when choosing was easy.

Optional accountability

Someone knows — quietly.

Choose a partner. If you miss bedtime, they get a short, factual note — over a channel they already use. Just knowing someone will see is usually enough to tip the night.

They never see your apps, your usage, or where you are. Only whether the phone went in. Accountability without surveillance.

Where it stands today

A few dozen people are living with it right now.

These are hand-built early units — real hardware on real nightstands, not a render. Rough around the edges, honestly. But everything the box is meant to do, it does, and people don't want to hand them back.

What's next

An MVP is an MVP. Today's units sense your phone with a simple push-button — it works, but it's fragile. The next version is a purpose-built case with USB-C charging and detection built in: plug the phone in to charge, and the box already knows it's there. Nothing to balance, nothing to miss. That's what we're building now.
