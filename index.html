Eudaimon.
What it is

Eudaimonia

yoo-dai-mon-EE-a  ·  Ancient Greek

Aristotle's word for flourishing — not a fleeting high, but the lifelong practice of living well.

He meant it as a verb: flourishing is something you do, daily, by acting like the person you mean to be. Eudaimon is built on that one idea — and it starts with the smallest, hardest rep of it: actually going to bed when you said you would.

Meet the device ↓

A bedside box that takes your phone at night, holds you to your own wind-down, and gives it back in the morning.

Eudaimon — the structure you build before you need it

Why I built it

Quick, honest one: I doomscrolled past my own bedtime for years. I tried it all — screen-time limits, app blockers, greyscale, deleting apps, leaving the phone in another room. Nothing held for more than a week. So I made it my mission to build the thing that finally would. After a lot of dead ends, this is what I want to show you.

— David, founder

The problem

The you at 11pm isn't the you who set the alarm at 8am. Same body, different will.

Willpower runs on your prefrontal cortex — the decision-making part right behind your forehead. It drains through the day, and by night it's nearly empty. Most apps fail because they ask your most depleted self to make the call, exactly when it can't.

Eudaimon flips it

Morning-you makes the rules. The box holds them when evening-you would break them.

A Ulysses contract in hardware: tie yourself to the mast while you're clear-headed, and let the box keep the rope.

When it comes to improving yourself, 80% is just getting to bed on time.

Win the night and the day mostly takes care of itself.

The night, walked through

It only shows up when you need it. Doing fine? It stays dark.

01

Wind-down

One gentle ping: ease off now. Not a buzzer — a soft chime you can wave away with a tap.

02

The evening routine

A short checklist you wrote yourself — each item negotiable or non-negotiable, so a hard night still ends in bed.

03

The phone goes in the box

Your phone goes in a real box that physically detects it. No tap or checkbox can fake the commitment.

04

If you stall, it shifts tone

It doesn't just get louder — gentle, then firm, then your own words on the screen. No "comply" button; you finish by doing the thing.

05

Morning gives it back

Light before the alarm, then a small task to prove you're up — no snooze, no math. Then the phone is yours again.

Protective, not punitive

The sheepdog you hired this morning.

Not a cop. It catches you, never judges you, and stays dark and quiet when you're doing fine.

What testers feel most isn't willpower. It's relief from the decision — already made, hours ago, when it was easy.

Optional accountability

Someone knows — quietly.

Pick a partner. Miss bedtime, and they get a short, factual note over a channel they already use.

They never see your apps, your usage, or where you are — only whether the phone went in. Accountability without surveillance.
