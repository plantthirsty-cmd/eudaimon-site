What it is

Eudaimonia

yoo-dai-mon-EE-a  ·  Ancient Greek

Aristotle's word for flourishing — not a fleeting high, but the lifelong practice of living well.

He meant it as a verb — flourishing is something you do, daily, by acting like who you mean to be. Eudaimon starts with its smallest, hardest rep: going to bed when you said you would.

The behavioral psychology answer to doomscrolling.

Meet the device ↓

A bedside box that takes your phone at night, holds you to your own wind-down, and gives it back in the morning.

The problem

The you at 11pm isn't the you who set the alarm at 8am. Same body, different will.

If you have trouble putting your phone down and sticking to the routines you actually want to be doing, you are far from alone. It's one of our modern times' biggest issues — and yet we still lack real solutions or understanding. The data is clear: most doomscrolling happens at night, when your willpower is at its lowest. Fix the night, and you stand a fighting chance the next day.

How it works

How Eudaimon flips it

Most apps fail because they ask your most depleted self to make the call. Eudaimon does the opposite: you set your rules in the morning, when you're clear-headed — this is called a Ulysses contract — and a physical device holds them when evening-you would break them. And 80% of getting better is just getting to bed on time.

  • A sheepdog, not a cop — it catches you, never shames you, and stays quiet when you're fine.
  • Relief, not discipline — the calm of having already decided, hours ago, when it was easy.
  • Optional accountability — a partner gets a quiet note if you miss bedtime. Never your apps, usage, or location.

How it plays out

The night, walked through

While you're on track, it's just soft guidance — it only makes itself felt the moment you start to slip.

01

Wind-down

Time to wrap it up. A gentle reminder to send those last texts and do whatever you need to be ready to hand over your phone.

02

The evening routine

A short checklist you wrote yourself — each item negotiable or non-negotiable, so a hard night still ends in bed.

03

The phone goes in the box

Your phone goes in a real box that physically detects it. No tap or checkbox can fake the commitment.

04

If you stall, it becomes impossible to ignore

Eudaimon is a physical thing in your room. Through audio and visual cues it becomes increasingly hard to ignore, and there is no button to make it stop. The only way out is to do the thing you decided to do this morning.

05

Your morning is yours

Eudaimon is your alarm clock, so your phone can stay in the box for those precious few minutes where you stretch, breathe, and actually wake up. Then your phone is yours again.
